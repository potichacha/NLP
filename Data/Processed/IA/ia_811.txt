In GCSE Biology Trilogy (Combined Science), there are two main types of reproduction: sexual reproduction and asexual reproduction. Sexual Reproduction: Sexual reproduction involves the fusion of gametes (sex cells) from two different parents to produce offspring with a unique combination of genetic traits. The gametes are produced by meiosis, which halves the number of chromosomes in the cell. In animals, the male gamete is called a sperm, while the female gamete is called an egg or ovum. Fertilization occurs when a sperm penetrates and fuses with an egg to form a zygote. Sexual reproduction is common in plants and animals, including humans. Asexual Reproduction: Asexual reproduction involves the production of offspring from a single parent without the involvement of gametes. In asexual reproduction, the offspring are genetically identical to the parent, as there is no mixing of genetic material. Asexual reproduction occurs in many plants and some animals, such as bacteria, fungi, and some types of invertebrates. There are several different types of asexual reproduction, including: Binary Fission: This is a type of asexual reproduction seen in bacteria, where the cell divides into two identical daughter cells. Budding: This is a form of asexual reproduction where a new individual grows from a bud on the parent, such as in Hydra. Vegetative Reproduction: This involves the growth of new individuals from vegetative parts of a plant, such as leaves, roots, or stems. Examples include runners in strawberries and tubers in potatoes. Fragmentation: This is a type of asexual reproduction seen in some animals, where the parent organism breaks into fragments, and each fragment can grow into a new individual.
